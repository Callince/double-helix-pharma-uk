<p><strong>An MIA and a WDA cover different activities.</strong> A <strong>Manufacturer's/Importer's Authorisation (MIA)</strong> lets you manufacture, assemble, import and certify the release of medicines, under EU GMP and a Qualified Person. A <strong>Wholesale Dealer's Authorisation (WDA(H))</strong> lets you store, distribute, supply and export them, under EU GDP and a Responsible Person. Many companies hold one; some need both.</p>

<p>The two are easy to confuse because both are MHRA licences for handling medicines — but they sit on different sides of the supply chain, follow different standards, and require different named people. This guide explains each one, sets them side by side, and helps you work out which you actually need.</p>

<h2>What is an MIA (Manufacturer's/Importer's Authorisation)?</h2>
<p>An <strong>MIA</strong> authorises the manufacture, assembly or importation of licensed medicines, and the certification of each batch before it is released to the market. It is held against the standards of <strong>EU GMP (EudraLex Volume 4)</strong> and requires a named <strong>Qualified Person (QP)</strong> to certify batches. A related variant, the <strong>MIA(IMP)</strong>, covers investigational medicinal products for clinical trials.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Activities:</strong> manufacture, assembly, importation, QP batch certification and release.</li>
<li><strong>Standard:</strong> EU GMP — EudraLex Vol. 4 (and 21 CFR 210/211, ICH Q7–Q10 where relevant).</li>
<li><strong>Named person:</strong> Qualified Person (QP).</li>
</ul>

<h2>What is a WDA (Wholesale Dealer's Authorisation)?</h2>
<p>A <strong>WDA(H)</strong> authorises the wholesale distribution of human medicines — procuring, holding, storing, supplying and exporting them — but <strong>not</strong> their manufacture or batch release. It is held against <strong>EU GDP (2013/C 343/01)</strong> and requires a named <strong>Responsible Person (RP)</strong>. To import medicines into Great Britain from outside the UK under a WDA, you also need a <strong>Responsible Person for import (RPi)</strong>.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Activities:</strong> buying, holding, storing, supplying, exporting medicines.</li>
<li><strong>Standard:</strong> EU GDP (2013/C 343/01) and MHRA expectations.</li>
<li><strong>Named person:</strong> Responsible Person (RP); plus an RPi for importation.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Do you need an MIA, a WDA, or both?</h2>
<p>It depends on what you do with the product.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Manufacture or import and release</strong> medicines → you need an <strong>MIA</strong> (with a QP).</li>
<li><strong>Only buy, store and distribute</strong> finished, already-released medicines → you need a <strong>WDA(H)</strong> (with an RP).</li>
<li><strong>Both manufacture and distribute</strong> → you may need <strong>both</strong> licences, each with its own named person and quality system.</li>
</ul>
<p>Getting either site, dossier and team ready for the pre-licensing inspection is exactly what <a href="/site-readiness">MIA/WDA site readiness</a> is for.</p>

<h2>What about importing — MIA(IMP) and the RPi?</h2>
<p>Since Brexit, importing medicines into Great Britain has its own rules. Importing finished, licensed medicines from a country on the approved list for wholesale generally needs a <strong>WDA with an RPi</strong>, who confirms the appropriate checks (including QP certification or its equivalent) have taken place. Importing to manufacture or to perform QP certification needs an <strong>MIA</strong>. If you are unsure which route applies, a <a href="/contract-qp">contract QP, RP or RPi</a> can scope it with you.</p>

<h2>QP vs RP vs RPi: who must be named?</h2>
<p>Each licence has a different responsible individual, and a single person may hold more than one role if eligible.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Qualified Person (QP)</strong> — named on an MIA; certifies and releases each batch.</li>
<li><strong>Responsible Person (RP)</strong> — named on a WDA; ensures GDP compliance across distribution.</li>
<li><strong>RP for import (RPi)</strong> — named on a WDA; oversees the importation of medicines into GB.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How do you apply and prepare for the inspection?</h2>
<p>Both licences are granted by the MHRA following an application and a pre-licensing inspection of your site, quality system and named personnel. Preparing properly — a current Site Master File, a working <a href="/qms-implementation">Pharmaceutical Quality System</a>, and a team ready to host — is the difference between a clean grant and a stalled application. ## FAQ — paste each Q&A into the post's FAQ fields (auto-generates FAQPage schema)

1. **What is the difference between an MIA and a WDA?**
   An MIA (Manufacturer's/Importer's Authorisation) covers manufacturing, importing and QP batch release of medicines under EU GMP. A WDA(H) (Wholesale Dealer's Authorisation) covers storing, distributing and exporting already-released medicines under EU GDP. In short: an MIA makes or releases medicines; a WDA moves them. Different standards, different named persons.

2. **Can one company hold both an MIA and a WDA?**
   Yes. A company that both manufactures and distributes medicines often holds both. Each licence is assessed separately, follows its own standard (GMP for the MIA, GDP for the WDA), and requires its own named responsible individual — a Qualified Person for the MIA and a Responsible Person for the WDA.

3. **Do I need a QP for a WDA?**
   No. A WDA requires a Responsible Person (RP), not a Qualified Person. A QP is required for an MIA, where they certify and release batches. To import medicines into Great Britain under a WDA, you additionally need a Responsible Person for import (RPi).

4. **What is an RPi and when do I need one?**
   An RPi — Responsible Person for import — is named on a WDA and oversees the importation of medicines into Great Britain from outside the UK. You need one if you import licensed medicines for wholesale rather than manufacturing or QP-certifying them yourself. The RPi confirms appropriate checks, including QP certification or its equivalent.

5. **Which licence do I need to import medicines into the UK?**
   It depends on the activity. Importing finished, licensed medicines for wholesale generally requires a WDA with an RPi. Importing to manufacture or to perform QP certification requires an MIA. The correct route depends on your supply chain and what you do with the product on arrival.

6. **How long does it take to get an MIA or WDA?**
   Timelines vary with site readiness and MHRA scheduling, but expect several months from application to grant, including a pre-licensing inspection. The biggest variable is how prepared your site, quality system and named persons are — strong readiness work shortens the path considerably.
