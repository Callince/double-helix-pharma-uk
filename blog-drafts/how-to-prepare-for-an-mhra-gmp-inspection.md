<p><strong>Preparing for an MHRA GMP inspection</strong> means proving — on paper and on the floor — that your Pharmaceutical Quality System genuinely works. Start 8–12 weeks out: run a mock inspection or gap assessment, close open deviations and CAPAs, verify data integrity end to end, and brief every host. Walk in with a current Site Master File, controlled SOPs and a calm, rehearsed team.</p>

<p>An inspection is not a test you cram for the week before — it is the visible output of a quality system that has been working all year. This guide sets out a realistic, phased plan: what triggers an inspection, when to start, what inspectors actually look at, the findings that most often trip sites up, and how to handle the day itself and the response that follows.</p>

<h2>What triggers an MHRA GMP inspection?</h2>
<p>MHRA GMP inspections are usually one of three types, and the type shapes your preparation. A pre-licensing inspection asks whether your site can operate exactly as described in its licence application; a for-cause inspection drills into a specific problem.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Routine (risk-based)</strong> — periodic, with frequency set by your risk profile and inspection history.</li>
<li><strong>Pre-approval / pre-licensing</strong> — before a new MIA or MIA(IMP) is granted, or for a significant variation. This is where <a href="/site-readiness">MIA/WDA site readiness</a> work pays off.</li>
<li><strong>For-cause / triggered</strong> — prompted by a product defect, recall, complaint, whistle-blower report or a serious deviation.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How far in advance should you prepare?</h2>
<p>Begin <strong>at least 8–12 weeks before</strong> the expected date — earlier still if you have known gaps or it is your site's first inspection. The phased plan below keeps remediation, documentation and rehearsal from colliding in the final fortnight.</p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>When</th><th>Focus</th><th>Key outputs</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>8–12 weeks out</td><td>Gap assessment / mock inspection</td><td>Prioritised remediation plan; open-item register</td></tr>
<tr><td>4–6 weeks out</td><td>Remediation &amp; documentation</td><td>Closed CAPAs/deviations; current SOPs, SMF, VMP</td></tr>
<tr><td>1–2 weeks out</td><td>Rehearsal &amp; logistics</td><td>Host briefings; back-room set up; document index ready</td></tr>
<tr><td>Day of</td><td>Inspection management</td><td>Front-room discipline; scribe notes; daily wash-up</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<h2>What is a step-by-step MHRA inspection preparation plan?</h2>
<p>Work through these phases in order. Each builds the evidence an inspector expects to see.</p>
<h3>1. Run an independent gap assessment or mock inspection</h3>
<p>An external, inspection-grade <a href="/gmp-audit">GMP audit or mock inspection</a> finds the gaps before the regulator does. Use the same scope and rigour the MHRA will: assess against EU GMP (EudraLex Volume 4), and 21 CFR 210/211 and ICH Q7–Q10 where relevant. Pair it with our <a href="/blog/gmp-audit-readiness-checklist">GMP audit-readiness checklist</a> to self-score first.</p>
<h3>2. Close your open quality records</h3>
<ul>
<li>Bring <strong>deviations, OOS/OOT, complaints and recalls</strong> to a defined, justified status — no ageing, un-investigated records.</li>
<li>Demonstrate <strong>CAPA effectiveness</strong>, not just CAPA closure. An open or recurring CAPA is a magnet for findings.</li>
<li>Confirm <strong>change controls</strong> are assessed, approved and implemented with evidence.</li>
</ul>
<h3>3. Verify data integrity end to end</h3>
<p>Data integrity is the single most scrutinised area in modern inspections. Evidence that records are <strong>Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original and Accurate (ALCOA+)</strong> across paper and computerised systems — audit trails reviewed, access controls in place, no shared logins, validated systems to GAMP 5 and Annex 11.</p>
<h3>4. Refresh your core documentation</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Site Master File (SMF)</strong> current and consistent with what happens on the floor.</li>
<li><strong>Validation Master Plan (VMP)</strong>, qualification (URS/DQ/IQ/OQ/PQ) and ongoing process verification up to date.</li>
<li><strong>SOPs</strong> in version control, trained out, and actually followed — mismatches between SOP and practice are common, avoidable findings.</li>
</ul>
<h3>5. Confirm batch release and QP arrangements</h3>
<p>Ensure your batch-certification process, QP responsibilities and any technical/quality agreements are watertight. If you lack permanent cover, arrange a <a href="/contract-qp">contract Qualified Person</a> well before the inspection — not during it.</p>
<h3>6. Brief and rehearse your team</h3>
<p>Confident, honest hosts who answer the question asked — and only the question asked — are worth more than any binder. Run a dry run; agree who speaks to what, how documents are retrieved, and how the back room supports the front room.</p>

<h2>What do MHRA inspectors actually look at?</h2>
<p>Expect the inspection to follow the strength of your <a href="/qms-implementation">Pharmaceutical Quality System (ICH Q10)</a>. The recurring focus areas are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Quality management — deviations, CAPA, change control, product quality reviews, self-inspection (EU GMP Chapter 9).</li>
<li>Data integrity and computerised systems (ALCOA+, Annex 11, GAMP 5).</li>
<li>Batch release, QP certification and OOS/OOT handling.</li>
<li>Sterile manufacturing controls where applicable (EU GMP <strong>Annex 1</strong>) — contamination control strategy, environmental monitoring.</li>
<li>Premises, equipment and utilities qualification and maintenance.</li>
<li>Personnel training, competence and hygiene.</li>
<li>Supplier, CMO and material oversight.</li>
</ul>

<h2>What are the most common MHRA inspection findings — and how do you avoid them?</h2>
<p>Most deficiencies cluster in a handful of areas. Fix these before the inspector arrives.</p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr><th>Common finding</th><th>How to avoid it</th></tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr><td>Data integrity gaps (audit trails, shared logins)</td><td>Review audit trails, enforce unique access, validate systems to Annex 11 / GAMP 5</td></tr>
<tr><td>Weak or ineffective CAPA</td><td>Address root cause; verify effectiveness; close on evidence, not on time</td></tr>
<tr><td>Ageing, un-investigated deviations</td><td>Triage by risk; investigate promptly; trend and escalate</td></tr>
<tr><td>SOP vs practice mismatch</td><td>Walk the process; align the SOP to reality; retrain</td></tr>
<tr><td>Inadequate supplier/CMO oversight</td><td>Risk-based qualification, current quality agreements, audit programme</td></tr>
</tbody>
</table>

<h2>How do you manage the inspection on the day?</h2>
<ul>
<li>Run a disciplined <strong>front room / back room</strong>: the front room hosts the inspector; the back room retrieves documents and prepares answers.</li>
<li>Keep a <strong>scribe</strong> recording every request, question and commitment.</li>
<li>Be <strong>honest and concise</strong> — answer the question, show the evidence, don't speculate or over-share.</li>
<li>Hold a <strong>daily wash-up</strong> to address emerging issues and, where possible, correct minor items before close-out.</li>
</ul>

<h2>How do you respond to inspection findings?</h2>
<p>Findings are graded <strong>critical, major or other</strong>. Respond with a clear, time-bound CAPA plan that addresses root cause and includes effectiveness checks — and meet the MHRA's response deadlines. A strong, evidence-led response can be the difference between a smooth outcome and an escalation.</p>

1. **How long does it take to prepare for an MHRA GMP inspection?**
   Allow at least 8–12 weeks, and longer for a first inspection or where known gaps exist. The bulk of that time goes on remediation — closing deviations and CAPAs, fixing data-integrity issues and aligning SOPs with practice — not on last-minute documentation. A mock inspection early in the window keeps surprises out of the final fortnight.

2. **What is the most common reason sites fail an MHRA inspection?**
   Data integrity and ineffective CAPA are the most frequent serious findings. Inspectors look for evidence that records are ALCOA+ compliant and that corrective actions actually address root cause and prevent recurrence — not simply that records were closed on time.

3. **What is the difference between a routine and a for-cause inspection?**
   A routine inspection is a periodic, risk-based review of your overall GMP compliance. A for-cause inspection is triggered by a specific event — a complaint, recall, defect report or serious deviation — and drills deeply into that issue and the system weaknesses behind it.

4. **Should we run a mock inspection before the real one?**
   Yes. An independent mock inspection or gap assessment, run to the same standard the MHRA applies, is the single most effective preparation. It surfaces gaps while you still have time to fix them and lets your team rehearse hosting under realistic pressure.

5. **Do we need a Qualified Person named for the inspection?**
   For an MIA, a QP must be in place to certify and release batches, and the inspection will examine those arrangements. If you do not have permanent cover, arrange a contract QP well in advance so the role and responsibilities are established and evidenced before the inspector arrives.

6. **What happens after the inspection?**
   You receive graded findings (critical, major or other) and must submit a CAPA plan within the MHRA's stated timeframe. The response should address root cause, include effectiveness checks and realistic completion dates. A clear, evidence-led response is key to a positive outcome.
